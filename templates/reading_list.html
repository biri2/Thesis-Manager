HANK Foundations & Monetary Transmission

Core inequality/MPC transmission logic and decomposition language. This is your baseline household block and the foundation for D6.

Monetary Policy According to HANK

Greg Kaplan, Benjamin Moll, Giovanni L. Violante — 2018
CORE D0D1D2D6

This paper establishes the core HANK view of monetary transmission: heterogeneity and incomplete markets imply that policy works mainly through income, redistribution, and general-equilibrium effects, not the representative-agent intertemporal substitution channel. It rationalizes large MPCs and realistic wealth distributions.

Read Paper
Explanation

Kaplan, Moll, and Violante reframe monetary policy transmission in economies where households face idiosyncratic risk and incomplete insurance. In a representative-agent NK model, the main force is intertemporal substitution: changes in real rates shift consumption by altering Euler equations. In HANK, that channel is typically small because many households are near borrowing constraints or hold portfolios that make them insensitive to marginal interest rate changes. Instead, policy affects the economy through (i) income and employment (aggregate demand and labor demand), (ii) redistribution across households with different balance sheets (borrowers vs savers; high- vs low-MPC groups), and (iii) general-equilibrium feedbacks that change wages, profits, and asset prices. The key takeaway is methodological and quantitative: once you match realistic heterogeneity, the “who is affected” matters as much as “the average agent.” For your thesis, this paper provides the intellectual foundation to interpret climate disaster shocks and monetary responses: disasters shift incomes and constraints unevenly, and monetary stabilization will redistribute purchasing power across the wealth distribution. It also gives you a clean benchmark to validate your HANK block before introducing financial intermediaries and rare-disaster nonlinearity.

Relevance
  • D0 (Model spec): the baseline household heterogeneity logic; which channels matter in HANK.
  • D1 (Calibration targets): discipline via wealth distribution and MPC moments (liquid constraints, marginal propensities).
  • D2 (Baseline validation): qualitative IRFs and transmission patterns to replicate before adding climate/banks.
  • D6 (Decomposition): a template for attributing aggregate effects to distributional channels.

Monetary Policy and the Redistribution Channel

Adrien Auclert — 2019
HIGH D0D6

Auclert provides a structured decomposition of monetary policy effects into redistribution channels—how interest rate changes reallocate resources across borrowers/savers and across agents with different portfolios, MPCs, and exposures. It turns “heterogeneity matters” into measurable components usable in quantitative work.

Read Paper
Explanation

Auclert’s contribution is to make redistribution a first-class object in monetary analysis. In heterogeneous-agent settings, a change in nominal (and hence real) interest rates affects agents differently depending on their net nominal positions (debt vs assets), the maturity structure of portfolios, and their sensitivity of consumption to income (MPC). Auclert organizes these effects into interpretable components that connect individual balance sheets to aggregate outcomes. Conceptually, the paper clarifies that policy is not just “lower rates stimulate spending” via a representative Euler equation; policy also changes who receives resources—through interest payments, valuation effects, and wage/profit responses—and those distributional shifts can be expansionary or contractionary depending on which groups have higher MPCs. For your thesis, this is directly useful once you introduce climate rare disasters: disasters can push more households toward liquidity constraints and raise the aggregate MPC, so the redistribution channel becomes stronger and more state-dependent. Auclert’s decomposition helps you write the results section with precision: you can explain whether monetary policy stabilizes primarily by boosting labor income, by reallocating resources toward high-MPC households, or by affecting asset valuations that matter for constrained groups. In short, it’s your “interpretation toolkit” for D6.

Relevance
  • D0 (Model spec): channel taxonomy (who gains/loses when rates move; why MPC heterogeneity amplifies).
  • D2 (Baseline validation): interpret HANK monetary IRFs beyond “aggregate demand falls.”
  • D6 (Decomposition): explicit accounting framework to attribute amplification to redistribution vs other channels.

The Wealthy Hand-to-Mouth

Greg Kaplan, Giovanni L. Violante, Justin Weidner — 2014
HIGH D1

This paper documents that many households with substantial net worth still behave “hand-to-mouth” because wealth is illiquid. The liquid/illiquid asset split generates high MPCs even among asset holders and reshapes how fiscal and monetary policies transmit in HANK models.

Read Paper
Explanation

Kaplan, Violante, and Weidner show that “wealthy” in net worth is not the same as “liquid.” Many households hold significant illiquid assets (housing, retirement accounts, business equity) while maintaining low liquid balances (cash, checking). As a result, they cannot smoothly respond to shocks by adjusting consumption based on lifetime resources; they respond like hand-to-mouth households in the short run because the marginal dollar of liquid resources is scarce. This observation resolves a key empirical puzzle: why measured MPCs are high even though aggregate wealth is large. It also motivates HANK model structures that distinguish liquid and illiquid assets (or, more generally, enforce effective liquidity constraints). For your thesis, this paper is essential as an empirical anchor: climate disasters and associated financial stress can tighten liquidity, increase the share of constrained households, and raise the aggregate MPC—mechanisms that amplify demand contractions. It also informs calibration decisions: the correct object to match is not only total wealth inequality but liquidity inequality and the prevalence of “wealthy hand-to-mouth” behavior. That discipline is critical if you want your disaster amplification results to be credible rather than driven by arbitrary heterogeneity choices.

Relevance
  • D1 (Calibration targets): liquid wealth distributions, fraction hand-to-mouth, MPC by liquid wealth bins.
  • D0 (Model spec): motivation for liquidity constraints/portfolio structure (liquid vs illiquid assets).
  • D2 (Validation): sanity checks: your model should reproduce high MPCs consistent with liquidity scarcity.
Methods Spine: Nonlinear Solution, Sequence-Space, and Estimation

The feasibility stack: how you solve nonlinear HANK under large shocks, validate it, and (optionally) add estimation or neural surrogates.

HANK on Speed: Robust Nonlinear Solutions using AD

Gregor Boehl — 2024
CORE D2D5

A practical paper on solving nonlinear HANK models fast and robustly using automatic differentiation and sequence-space Newton methods. It emphasizes implementation details, convergence diagnostics, and computational design choices that make large-shock transition dynamics feasible and reproducible.

Read Paper
Explanation

Boehl’s contribution is to make nonlinear HANK transition dynamics operational rather than aspirational. The key idea is to exploit automatic differentiation (AD) to obtain accurate derivatives (Jacobians) required by Newton-type solvers, while working in “sequence space”: instead of solving point-by-point for time-t variables only, you solve directly for entire time paths that satisfy equilibrium conditions. This approach is particularly well-suited to HANK models because heterogeneity introduces large state objects (distributions) and highly nonlinear feedback loops that can break naive shooting or local-linear methods—especially under large shocks such as rare disasters.

The paper’s value for your thesis is methodological discipline: it pushes you toward a workflow with clear objects (steady state, transition path, residuals), explicit convergence criteria, and robust debugging tools (e.g., checking equilibrium residuals along the path, monitoring dampening/step sizes, and stress-testing solver stability). It also encourages modular model design, where the household block and aggregation are written in a way that the solver can repeatedly evaluate them quickly. For your project, this is the backbone for credible nonlinear disaster counterfactuals and policy-rule experiments.

Relevance
  • D2 (Baseline solved): stable steady state + validated transition solver workflow.
  • D5 (Nonlinear experiments): large-shock, state-dependent disaster simulations you can actually run.

Using the Sequence-Space Jacobian to Solve HANK Models

Adrien Auclert, Bence Bardóczy, Matthew Rognlie, Ludwig Straub — 2021
CORE D2D7

Introduces the sequence-space Jacobian (SSJ) method: a fast way to solve and estimate heterogeneous-agent models by computing general-equilibrium impulse responses via Jacobians of sequences. It turns HANK solution and estimation into linear algebra built on block derivatives.

Read Paper
Explanation

The SSJ framework reframes heterogeneous-agent general equilibrium as a problem of mapping sequences of aggregate shocks into sequences of aggregate outcomes. The technical bottleneck in HANK models is that individual decisions depend on expectations and distributions, which makes “standard” linearization expensive or opaque. Auclert et al. show that you can compute a model’s dynamic response efficiently by assembling Jacobians for each block—households, firms, policy rule—and then solving the resulting system for equilibrium sequences.

Practically, the paper gives you a clear decomposition: (i) compute partial-equilibrium responses of household aggregates to sequences of prices/income (the HA block Jacobians), (ii) compute Jacobians for the remaining GE blocks (Phillips curve, monetary policy, market clearing), and (iii) close the model by solving for sequences that satisfy equilibrium. This is powerful because it both speeds computation and improves transparency: you can inspect which block contributes to which response.

For your thesis, SSJ is valuable even if your final results are nonlinear. It provides a defensible baseline for validation (your HANK block and aggregation should reproduce known linear IRFs), and it supports limited estimation strategies (matching IRFs or moments) without needing to fully simulate nonlinear distributions every time. It also structures how you present your mechanisms in the write-up.

Relevance
  • D2 (Baseline solved): benchmark solution method to validate your implementation and IRFs.
  • D7 (Optional estimation): provides a credible route to likelihood/moment-based estimation with heterogeneous agents.

Estimating DSGE Models: Recent Advances

Jesús Fernández-Villaverde, Pablo Guerrón-Quintana — 2020
HIGH D1D7

A high-level but concrete map of modern DSGE estimation: Bayesian workflows, particle filters, identification issues, computational constraints, and practical pitfalls. It clarifies what “credible estimation” means in practice and why nonlinear models raise new challenges.

Read Paper
Explanation

This paper is the “methodological governance” reference: it helps you design an estimation strategy that is both credible and feasible. Fernández-Villaverde and Guerrón-Quintana review the standard Bayesian DSGE toolkit—priors, likelihood evaluation, MCMC/SMC—and then focus on the two practical constraints that matter most for your project: (1) nonlinearities and occasionally binding constraints (e.g., disaster states, financial constraints, ELB-like features) complicate likelihood evaluation; and (2) high-dimensional state spaces (as in HANK) stress standard filtering and simulation methods.

The paper is particularly useful as a checklist of failure modes: weak identification, overly-informative priors driving results, measurement error and model misspecification, and the trade-off between model richness and estimability. It also emphasizes the need to align data moments with model mechanisms—exactly what you want when calibrating wealth/MPC targets, leverage/spreads, and macro volatilities.

For your thesis, this reference supports a disciplined scope: estimate a small subset of parameters if you can, calibrate the rest, and communicate clearly why. It also helps you justify using modern computational accelerators (e.g., neural surrogates) as practical tools rather than “new econometrics,” aligning with your stated preference to use novel ideas without inventing them.

Relevance
  • D1 (Calibration/targets): helps you justify what you calibrate vs estimate and why.
  • D7 (Optional estimation): frames feasible estimation choices and communicates limitations professionally.

Estimating Nonlinear HANK Models with Neural Networks

Hanno Kase, Leonardo Melosi, Matthias Rottner — 2025
CORE D7

Proposes an “amortized” neural-network approach to solve and estimate nonlinear HANK models globally: parameters enter as inputs to policy functions, enabling fast repeated evaluation for likelihood-based estimation even with strong nonlinearities such as ZLB-like constraints.

Read Paper
Explanation

Kase–Melosi–Rottner attack the core computational obstacle in nonlinear HANK estimation: you typically need to solve the model repeatedly for many parameter draws, which is prohibitively slow when the model is high-dimensional and nonlinear. Their approach uses neural networks as global approximators of policy functions, with a key twist: structural parameters are treated as additional inputs. This “parameterized policy function” design lets the network learn how optimal decisions change across both states and parameter values, effectively amortizing the cost of solving the model.

Once trained, the network can be used to simulate the economy quickly under many parameter combinations, making likelihood-based or moment-based estimation substantially more feasible. The paper also illustrates how this approach can handle nonlinear features that complicate traditional linearized estimation—exactly the sort of nonlinearity you face with disaster states and financial constraints.

For your thesis, this paper gives you a concrete, defensible path to integrating deep learning without drifting into “novel estimation theory.” You can position the NN as a computational device: it accelerates model evaluation for either (i) limited likelihood-based estimation of a small parameter subset, or (ii) simulated method of moments (SMM) where you match a handful of macro and macro-finance moments. It also helps you define diagnostics: training loss on equilibrium residuals, out-of-sample accuracy across parameter draws, and stability tests around crisis/disaster regimes.

Relevance
  • D7 (NN surrogate/estimation): primary blueprint for your deep-learning layer.
  • D2/D5 (Computation): motivates architecture choices that keep nonlinear experiments fast.

Deep Learning for Solving Economic Models

Jesús Fernández-Villaverde — 2025
HIGH D7

A survey and methodological guide on using deep neural networks to solve DSGE and related macro models globally. It explains why deep learning can mitigate the curse of dimensionality, outlines training strategies, and emphasizes diagnostics and error measurement.

Read Paper
Explanation

Fernández-Villaverde’s goal is to translate deep learning from a generic tool into a macro model solution technology. The central argument is pragmatic: many economically relevant models are high-dimensional and nonlinear, and classical solution methods can become infeasible or fragile. Deep networks offer flexible function approximation that can be trained either on simulated solution data (supervised learning) or by minimizing equilibrium residuals directly (equation-error or “physics-informed” style approaches).

For your project, the relevance is twofold. First, it supports the choice of global methods: rare disasters and financial constraints generate state dependence and occasional binding regimes where local linear approximations can be misleading. Second, it helps you design the “surrogate layer” cleanly if you pursue it: define the state vector carefully, choose normalization, ensure training covers the relevant regions of the state space (especially crisis/disaster states), and evaluate accuracy using economic diagnostics (Euler errors, equilibrium residuals, and stability of simulated moments).

This paper also helps you write the methods section with credibility. You can describe deep learning not as “black-box prediction,” but as an approximation to equilibrium objects subject to strict economic constraints. If you keep D7 optional, this reference still strengthens your positioning: your core results rely on modern nonlinear computation, and deep learning is a scalable extension for speeding counterfactuals or enabling limited estimation.

Relevance
  • D7 (Optional surrogate): provides the macro-appropriate DL framing and vocabulary.
  • D2/D5 (Nonlinear solution narrative): justifies why global approximations matter under rare disasters and binding constraints.

Deep Learning for Solving Dynamic Economic Models

Lilia Maliar, Serguei Maliar, Pablo Winant — 2021
SUPPORTING D7

A practical guide to implementing deep learning solution methods for dynamic economic models, focusing on training design, normalization, sampling of state spaces, and accuracy diagnostics. It’s especially useful for avoiding common implementation pitfalls in DL-based solvers.

Read Paper
Explanation

Maliar–Maliar–Winant focus on the engineering realities that determine whether deep learning solution methods work in practice. Even if the conceptual approach is sound, macro models can fail DL training because the state space is poorly sampled, variables are badly scaled, constraints are not enforced, or accuracy is evaluated with metrics that do not correspond to equilibrium quality.

The paper’s key contribution for you is methodological hygiene: it emphasizes normalization of inputs/outputs, careful construction of training samples (including the “rare but important” regions—exactly your disaster/crisis states), and robust diagnostics. Rather than reporting only training loss, it encourages checking economic error metrics such as Euler equation errors, violations of market clearing, and stability of simulated moments. It also discusses the trade-offs between different training regimes (e.g., simulation-based sampling vs grid-like coverage) and the importance of ensuring the approximator generalizes rather than memorizes local regions.

In your thesis, this paper is most valuable if you build a surrogate or NN-based solver (D7). It tells you what to log, what to plot, and how to convince a skeptical reader that your neural approximation is economically meaningful. Even if you do not implement D7, it can inform your “optional extension” section and help you argue that deep learning is feasible and disciplined when used with the right diagnostics.

Relevance
  • D7 (Surrogate implementation): concrete training and diagnostics guidance.
  • D2/D5 (Reliability): reinforces how to evaluate solution quality beyond “loss goes down.”
Climate Tail Risk, Rare Disasters & Monetary Policy Trade-offs

How disaster risk and realizations change the natural rate, inflation–output trade-offs, and the effectiveness/cost of stabilization.

Rare Disasters, the Natural Interest Rate and Monetary Policy

Antonio Cantelmo — 2022
CORE D0D4D5

Cantelmo studies how rare-disaster risk (even before any disaster occurs) shifts the natural real rate and alters macro dynamics. Disaster probability affects precautionary behavior and equilibrium pricing, changing the monetary policy problem relative to standard New Keynesian environments.

Read Paper
Explanation

The central idea is that rare disasters introduce a “tail-risk wedge” into macro equilibrium. In standard models, the natural rate and stabilization trade-offs are driven mainly by expected growth and preference shocks with thin tails. When the distribution has a low-probability disaster state, households and firms internalize that future marginal utility may spike in disaster times, increasing precautionary motives and changing asset pricing. Even if the disaster never occurs in-sample, the possibility of a disaster can depress the natural rate and alter equilibrium quantities—especially consumption/saving choices and pricing of risky claims. For monetary policy, this matters because a Taylor-rule designed around normal fluctuations can mis-handle environments where risk premia and precautionary forces move strongly with state variables (e.g., perceived disaster probability). In your thesis, this paper gives you a clean conceptual and quantitative rationale for distinguishing (i) shocks to disaster probability (“risk shocks”) from (ii) disaster realizations (“jump shocks”), and for studying how the optimal (or welfare-improving) degree of inflation stabilization may differ across these cases. It also motivates nonlinear solution methods: the economy’s responses can be highly state-dependent when rare events are present, and linear approximations risk understating both risk effects and policy trade-offs.

Relevance
  • D4 (Disaster risk vs realization): Direct template for your disaster-risk vs disaster-realization distinction.
  • D5 (Policy experiments): for policy-rule experiments that separate “risk effects” from “realization effects”.

Monetary Policy Design with Recurrent Climate Shocks

Gazi Kara, Arvind Thakoor — 2023
HIGH D4D5

Kara and Thakoor model climate disasters as recurrent, largely supply-side disturbances that raise inflation while lowering output, complicating stabilization. They analyze monetary policy rules in a climate-shock environment and show that standard prescriptions can perform poorly as shocks recur.

Read Paper
Explanation

This paper is valuable because it formalizes, in a relatively standard New Keynesian DSGE setting, the core “climate shocks look like adverse supply shocks” intuition: disasters reduce effective productivity or destroy capital, pushing marginal costs up, generating inflationary pressure, while simultaneously depressing output. This creates a sharper inflation–output trade-off than demand-driven downturns and forces the central bank into uncomfortable choices. The “recurrent” aspect matters: even if each shock is temporary, repeated shocks can keep the economy in a near-permanent stabilization problem, making policy design a medium-run issue rather than a one-off response. For your thesis, this paper anchors the climate-monetary narrative: you can reproduce their baseline results as a sanity check and then show how adding household heterogeneity (HANK) changes the demand side (high-MPC households, liquidity constraints) and how adding leveraged intermediation changes investment/spreads. That contrast is exactly your contribution: you do not merely restate the NK trade-off; you explain why it becomes more nonlinear and more state-dependent once distributional demand channels and financial constraints interact with climate shocks. Practically, you can map their climate-shock specification into your D4 module, then replicate their policy-rule comparisons as a benchmark set of figures before moving to the integrated model for D5.

Relevance
  • D4 (Climate benchmark): your RANK/NK climate benchmark.
  • D5 (Comparison point): gives a disciplined baseline trade-off to compare against your integrated HANK + finance model.

Solution Methods for Models with Rare Disasters

Jesús Fernández-Villaverde, Oren Levintal — 2018
HIGH D4D5

Fernández-Villaverde and Levintal explain why rare disasters strain standard perturbation methods and can generate large approximation errors. They discuss robust solution strategies for models with low-probability, high-impact states, emphasizing accuracy in tails and regime switching.

Read Paper
Explanation

Rare disasters are a worst-case scenario for local approximation methods: policy functions and equilibrium conditions can be smooth almost everywhere but change sharply in regions of the state space associated with disaster risk or disaster realization. Standard perturbation approaches approximate around a deterministic steady state and can miss (or mis-measure) the contribution of tails to expectations, risk premia, and precautionary dynamics. This paper’s practical contribution is to clarify what fails and why: even if the disaster probability is tiny, the payoffs and marginal utilities in disaster states can be so extreme that they matter for pricing and for equilibrium, making “small probability” not equivalent to “small effect.” For your thesis, this provides the strongest, cleanest rationale for adopting nonlinear tools (e.g., automatic differentiation + Newton methods, sequence-space methods, or global approximations) when you incorporate a disaster regime. It also guides how to implement the disaster process credibly: discrete Markov switching, jump processes, or hybrid schemes, and how to assess approximation quality (e.g., errors in Euler equations, pricing kernels, or tail-state moments). Importantly, it supports your key claim that disaster dynamics are not scaled-up normal shocks—the computational method itself must respect that. In writing, cite it when defending your modeling choice (“rare disasters require methods that remain accurate in tail states”), and in practice, use it as a checklist for diagnostics: do your computed paths respect equilibrium conditions in both normal and disaster regimes?

Relevance
  • D4 (Methods justification): justification for disaster-state modeling.
  • D5 (Nonlinear tools): rationale for using nonlinear/global solution tools.

The Macroeconomic Impact of Climate Change

Adrien Bilal, Diego R. Känzig — 2025
HIGH D1D4

Bilal and Känzig provide empirical evidence on how temperature measures translate into macroeconomic outcomes, distinguishing global and local temperature variation. Their results discipline the magnitude and persistence of climate-related macro effects and help map climate variables into structural shocks.

Read Paper
Explanation

The main value of this paper for your thesis is empirical discipline. Structural climate DSGEs (and especially rare-disaster climate variants) can be vulnerable to “free calibration”: if you choose disaster size/frequency without strong external evidence, results can look mechanically large or small. Bilal–Känzig help constrain that by quantifying macro responses to temperature changes and by clarifying whether global temperature dynamics or local deviations are the right driver for macro aggregates. This distinction matters for model design: global temperature may proxy for broader climate regimes and correlated global shocks (commodity prices, supply chains, financial conditions), while local temperature captures regional production or sector impacts. This reference is therefore a high-quality theoretical anchor for the “nonlinear amplification” narrative in your results section.
