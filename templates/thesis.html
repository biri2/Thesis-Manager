1) Executive Summary

Climate change increases macroeconomic tail risk: low-probability, high-severity disasters (hurricanes, floods, wildfires) can generate large and persistent output losses. The core thesis idea is that tail events do not propagate through the economy like scaled-up normal shocks because they interact with (i) household balance sheets and precautionary behavior and (ii) leveraged financial intermediaries.

The thesis proposes a nonlinear HANK-DSGE model with nominal rigidities and a central bank rule, augmented with an intermediary sector (“experts”/banks) subject to a leverage (skin-in-the-game) constraint. The model is designed to produce a double amplification:

  • Inequality/MPC channel: heterogeneous households (borrowing constraints, wealth distribution) imply that income/wealth shocks hit high-MPC agents hardest, amplifying aggregate demand contractions.
  • Financial accelerator channel: disasters erode intermediary net worth, tighten leverage constraints, widen spreads, and depress investment/output—creating feedback loops.

Methodologically, the thesis relies on modern nonlinear solution tools (automatic differentiation and sequence-space style solvers) and, if feasible, an ML layer (neural network surrogate) to enable extensive scenario analysis and/or estimation.

Thesis claim (in one line): The macro impact of climate rare disasters is state-dependent and strongly amplified when inequality-driven MPC heterogeneity interacts with leveraged intermediation—changing the monetary policy trade-off relative to representative-agent climate DSGEs.

3) The Literature Gap (What Your Thesis Fills)

The literature already contains partial overlaps (two components at a time), but rarely the full integration. The project proposal explicitly identifies the missing combination: HANK + climate rare disasters + leveraged intermediaries.

What exists (partial overlap)
Common in isolation
  • Climate disasters + monetary DSGE (often representative-agent): captures trade-offs but misses inequality and MPC channels.
  • HANK models (monetary transmission + redistribution): strong on distribution, weaker on climate tail events and disaster-state nonlinearity.
  • Financial accelerator / intermediary models: strong on spreads and crises, typically not embedded with household heterogeneity and climate-disaster processes simultaneously.
What is missing (your contribution)
Core gap

A quantitative framework where a climate rare-disaster process simultaneously (i) reshapes household balance sheets and consumption via MPC heterogeneity and (ii) stresses leveraged intermediaries, producing endogenous spreads and investment collapse, all within a New Keynesian monetary environment.

Why now (feasibility)
Method-enabled

The thesis is feasible in 2025 because modern methods (sequence-space ideas, automatic differentiation Newton solvers, and neural network surrogates) make nonlinear HANK-style models tractable for extensive scenario analysis and potentially estimation.

Logic Chain: Currently, integrated models are missing (Gap) -> This asks how they interact (RQ1) and how much creates amplification (RQ2). The representation bias leads to inaccurate trade-offs (RQ3).

4) Model Design: Blocks, State Variables, and Mechanisms

The model is intentionally modular: each block has precedent, but the novelty comes from integrating them and quantifying interactions.

Households (HANK)

  • Continuum of households with incomplete markets, idiosyncratic income risk, borrowing constraints, and heterogeneous MPCs.
  • Key output objects: consumption policy functions by wealth/income state; distributional responses to shocks.

Experts / Banks (Intermediation)

  • Small measure of experts intermediating deposits into productive capital, subject to leverage/pledgeability constraints.
  • Key output objects: intermediary net worth, leverage, spreads/credit wedge, investment sensitivity in stress states.

Climate rare-disaster process

  • Rare event modeled as a discrete state (Markov/Poisson-style): sharp productivity drop and/or capital destruction; also consider time-varying disaster risk as an extension.
  • Key output objects: ex-ante effects (precautionary saving, lower r*) vs ex-post realized disaster path.

Nominal rigidities + monetary policy

  • New Keynesian Phillips curve / sticky prices; central bank sets nominal rate by Taylor rule; optional effective lower bound as a later extension.
  • Key output objects: inflation-output trade-off under disaster risk/realization; welfare proxy comparisons across policy rules.
Core mechanism to highlight in the write-up: the disaster shock hits supply directly (TFP/capital) but also generates demand contractions through precautionary and distributional channels; the intermediary constraint translates asset value losses into a credit crunch; these channels reinforce each other in “bad” states.

5) Solution & (Optional) Estimation Strategy

The thesis is built around a pragmatic computational plan: (i) solve/validate the nonlinear model for large shocks and (ii) optionally add an ML-based surrogate/estimation layer.

Nonlinear solution (core)

  • Use automatic differentiation and sequence-space style Newton methods to compute transition paths for large shocks (disasters).
  • Validation: residual diagnostics, Euler errors, and benchmarking against simpler versions of the model.

Neural networks (optional but coherent)

  • Neural nets can approximate DSGE/HANK policy functions and handle high-dimensional state spaces; methods include supervised training and equation-error minimization (“unsupervised” equilibrium nets).
  • Optional: train a surrogate mapping from states/shocks to outcomes to accelerate scenario analysis and allow partial estimation.

Empirical component (calibration + optional estimation)

  • Baseline: disciplined calibration (disaster frequency/severity; bank leverage; HANK wealth/MPC moments) + moment fit table.
  • Optional: estimate a small subset of parameters using moments or approximate likelihood methods enabled by ML tools.
Scope control rule: The ML layer is an extension. The thesis is already strong if it delivers: nonlinear solution + double-amplification decomposition + monetary policy experiments.
Optional alternative angle (keep optional): replacing “fully rational” consumption-saving rules with reinforcement learning agents can model bounded rationality and learning dynamics, but it increases scope and should only be pursued if the core model is already stable.
